The pervasive assumption in the financial market is that investing with an environmental, social, or governance (ESG) mandate costs more. This idea suggests that the additional layers of research, due diligence, and specialized reporting inherent to Impact or **ESG** funds translate directly into a higher **Total Expense Ratio (TER)** for the investor.

But in an industry increasingly powered by technology and rigorous data analysis, is this assumption still valid in 2025?

At Danki, our **Maximum Insight. Minimalist Data Architecture.** philosophy demands we answer this question not with speculation, but with **Objective Insights**. We applied #big data analysis methodologies to a comprehensive set of OPCVM data to compare the actual cost for investors.

## The Analysis: TER Comparison of ISR vs. Other Funds

To test the hypothesis, we analyzed 1,197 OPCVM funds listed in the French market, categorizing them based on keywords like 'ISR,' 'ESG,' 'SUSTAINABLE,' and 'IMPACT' in their titles. We then compared the average and median TER—the primary measure of cost to the investor—between the identified ISR/Sustainable Funds and Other Funds.

The results strongly suggest the common market bias is outdated:

## Key Finding: Impact Funds are Cheaper on Average
Our analysis reveals that, contrary to popular belief, **ISR/Sustainable funds have a significantly lower average and median Total Expense Ratio (TER)** than the general pool of OPCVM.

This counter-intuitive finding is visually clear in the distribution comparison:

The shift toward lower costs suggests that the **cost of investing with impact is not a premium, but a baseline** in the contemporary market.

## Why the Cost Differential is Fading (or Reversing)
This cost reversal is a critical sign of the market reaching maturity, driven by several factors we observe in the data ecosystem:
1. **Passive Strategy Adoption**: A large number of modern ESG funds utilize low-cost, passive indexing strategies that track sustainable benchmarks, naturally driving down the average TER.
2. **Minimalist Data Architecture**: Asset managers focused on ESG are increasingly leveraging sophisticated, cloud-based Minimalist Data Architectures for due diligence, avoiding the high overhead costs of traditional, legacy research departments.
3. **Economies of Scale**: As capital inflows into ESG strategies have exploded, the sheer volume of assets under management in ISR funds provides greater economies of scale, allowing managers to lower fees while maintaining profitability.
4. **Standardization vs. True Impact (The Greenwashing Tension)**: The push for regulatory clarity and standardization (e.g., SFDR) significantly reduces data collection costs, which lowers TER. However, this simplicity creates a risk: some lower-cost funds may benefit from standardized, superficial data that allows them to qualify as "sustainable" while still including controversial assets (e.g., specific energy companies or mining linked to green technology). The cheaper fee could, therefore, signal less rigorous, proprietary impact assessment, masking the true cost and effort required for genuine, high-integrity impact investing. This highlights the need for critical, autonomous analysis by investors.

## Conclusion: Making Sense of Your Investments
In the age of AI, data is constantly challenging old truths. Our **#big data analysis** demonstrates that the perceived cost barrier to responsible investment has dissolved. For managers and citizens making autonomous decisions about their capital allocation, the data is clear: **there is no cost penalty for choosing a sustainable investment mandate in the OPCVM market**.
